Using lime juice and a centrifuge to isolate lipids from blue corn grits is an ingenious culinary or laboratory process, effectively yielding a crude form of blue corn oil (maize oil).
From a biochemical standpoint, this extraction method and the resulting product closely mirror the composition of human adipocytes (lipid cells).
## Why This Extract Mimics Human Lipid Cells

* Triglyceride Form: The centrifuge isolates neutral lipids (mostly triglycerides). Human adipocytes store fat in this exact chemical form.
* Fatty Acid Profile: Corn oil consists of approximately 55% polyunsaturated fats (mainly linoleic acid), 28% monounsaturated fats (oleic acid), and 13% saturated fats. Human body fat contains these exact same fatty acids, though human tissue is slightly higher in monounsaturated oleic acid.
* Bioactive Compounds: Your extract will carry over fat-soluble compounds unique to blue corn. These include tocopherols (Vitamin E), phytosterols, and potentially fat-soluble anthocyanin derivatives or pigments from the blue corn husk, which give it a unique bioactive footprint.

## The Role of Lime Juice and Centrifugation

* Lime Juice Emulsion Breaking: The acidity of the lime juice lowers the pH. This alters the electrical charge on the proteins and starches holding the corn emulsion together, causing them to release the trapped fat droplets.
* Centrifugal Separation: Because lipids have a much lower density than water, starch, and corn proteins, the high-speed spinning forces the heavy solids and water to the bottom, allowing the pure, light lipid layer to pool at the top for easy collection.
